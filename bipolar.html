What is Bipolar 1 Disorder?

Bipolar 1 Disorder is a mental health condition characterized by extreme mood swings, including episodes of mania and depression. This webpage aims to provide an in-depth understanding, resources, and support.

Symptoms

  • Increased energy and activity: Feeling highly energetic and restless, often leading to an increase in activity levels.
  • Decreased need for sleep: Feeling little to no need for sleep, sometimes for days at a time.
  • Racing thoughts and impulsivity: Rapidly shifting thoughts, sometimes leading to impulsive actions without considering consequences.
  • Exaggerated sense of self-importance: Feeling invincible or superior to others, which can lead to grandiose behavior.
  • Engaging in risky behaviors: Involvement in reckless activities like excessive spending, dangerous driving, or risky sexual behavior.
  • Increased talkativeness: Speaking quickly and excessively, with difficulty in stopping once started.
  • Difficulty concentrating or focusing: A scattered mind that struggles to focus on one task or thought for too long.
  • Heightened creativity: Feeling an unusual surge in creative ideas or activities, often leading to excessive planning or projects.
  • Feelings of sadness and hopelessness: A pervasive sense of sadness and despair that may last for weeks.
  • Lack of energy and interest in activities: Losing interest in things that used to bring joy or motivation.
  • Difficulty concentrating: Struggling to focus on tasks, making even simple activities feel overwhelming.
  • Changes in appetite: Significant weight loss or gain, often due to eating too much or too little.
  • Thoughts of death or suicide: Experiencing recurrent thoughts about death or feeling like life isn't worth living.
  • Feeling worthless or guilty: Overwhelming feelings of guilt, shame, or self-blame.
  • Difficulty sleeping (insomnia) or oversleeping: A marked change in sleep patterns, either sleeping too much or struggling to sleep.
  • Physical symptoms: Complaints of fatigue, aches, pains, or digestive problems without clear physical cause.
  • Delusions: Holding strong beliefs that are not based in reality, such as thinking one has extraordinary abilities or is being persecuted.
  • Hallucinations: Hearing voices or seeing things that are not present, often causing confusion or distress.
  • Disorganized thinking: Difficulty connecting thoughts logically, leading to incoherent or nonsensical speech.
  • Incoherent speech: Speaking in a way that is hard for others to follow, often jumping from one topic to another without clear transitions.
  • Paranoia: A persistent feeling of being watched, persecuted, or that others are conspiring against you.
  • Loss of touch with reality: Difficulty distinguishing between what is real and what is imagined.
  • Difficulty distinguishing between what’s real and what isn’t: Severe confusion about the nature of one’s thoughts and experiences.
